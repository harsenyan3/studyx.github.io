Research Evidence

Methodology, Findings, and Analysis

This page documents the usability data behind the redesign recommendations.

Methodology

Our team evaluated StudyX.ai using a pre-test questionnaire, a moderated usability test, and a post-test questionnaire. The pre-test questionnaire received six responses and was used to understand participants' academic level, current study habits, and familiarity with AI tools. The usability-test analysis below is based on five completed post-test questionnaires, which captured participants' reactions after using the product.

The participant pool included academically active users connected to Georgia Tech: undergraduate students, a graduate student, a dual enrollment student, and a professor or staff member. Pre-test responses showed that participants already used study supports such as flashcards, textbook problems, past exams, worksheets, office hours, Q&A, summarization, and concept explanation. Reported AI usage ranged from 2 to 9 on a 10-point scale, with an average of 4.5, meaning the group included both low-frequency and high-frequency AI users.

During usability testing, participants explored StudyX.ai's main workflows: landing page orientation, file upload, organization, quiz generation, quiz feedback, AI assistance, and summarization. The post-test questionnaire asked participants to evaluate the landing page and general UI, upload and organization features, quiz generation, quiz feedback clarity, AI assistance, summary quality, most useful features, and most frustrating parts of the experience.

We analyzed the post-test responses by coding repeated themes across the five respondents. Instead of treating a single comment as conclusive, we counted how many participants mentioned each usability problem or feature preference. This approach allowed us to connect design recommendations to observed patterns in the data, including navigation confusion, slow upload or processing feedback, weak AI assistance, and demand for a stronger quiz and tutoring workflow.

Findings

Finding 1: The landing page and navigation did not clearly guide users

Four of five post-test participants described the landing page or initial interface as difficult to understand, hard to navigate, or lacking onboarding. One participant said the site was "very purple" and difficult to know where to start, while another said the UI was clean but not intuitive. A third participant noted that the sign-up and payment elements felt too prominent, which distracted from the study workflow.

Finding 2: Upload, processing, and organization created uncertainty

Four of five participants identified problems with upload, processing speed, or organization. One participant wanted to upload multiple files instead of merging PDFs first. Another said the upload process was very slow compared with alternative tools. A third participant reported that drag-and-drop did not work and that previously uploaded files and generated summaries did not seem to remain available in the Library.

Finding 3: Quiz generation was the strongest existing feature, but feedback was inconsistent

Participants responded more positively to quiz generation than to most other features. Four of five participants described the quiz tool as useful, standard but functional, or one of the features they would return to. However, quiz feedback ratings varied widely, with scores of 3, 8, 5, 7, and 3 out of 10. The average rating was 5.2, showing that the quiz itself had value but the supporting feedback did not meet user expectations consistently.

Finding 4: AI assistance needed to be easier to find and more conversational

All five participants identified a problem or desired change related to AI assistance. Comments included that the AI assistant should be accessible from the start, that the ask-AI question tool was difficult to locate, that the response was incorrect, that the assistant was blocked by credits or a paid plan, and that the product needed an actual chatbot tutor. These responses directly support redesigning AI Tutor as a persistent chat experience with file upload and follow-up questions.

Finding 5: The visual design and feature set felt less academic than users expected

Several comments suggested that StudyX.ai did not feel sufficiently organized or academically focused. One participant called the interface "very purple," another asked for a more sophisticated interface, and another said the tools felt similar to other websites but less organized. These comments support the redesign's calmer green palette, clearer hierarchy, and more education-oriented visual identity.

Analysis

Figure 1 shows that AI assistance, landing/navigation clarity, and upload/processing feedback were the most frequently mentioned issues. Because four of five participants struggled with the initial interface or navigation, our redesign adds a clearer landing page and simplifies the sidebar into fewer, more purposeful categories. This directly responds to the evidence that users did not always know where to begin or how to locate the right tool.

Figure 2 shows that organization concerns appeared in three of five post-test responses, while upload interaction or processing issues appeared in multiple responses as well. These findings justify two redesign decisions: adding clearer upload progress feedback and redesigning the Library as an active workspace. The Library recommendation is especially supported by the comment that previous uploads and generated summaries were not easy to find again.

Figure 3 shows that quiz feedback received mixed ratings, with two participants rating it only 3 out of 10 while others rated it higher. This means quiz generation should not be removed; it was one of the product's strongest features. However, the surrounding learning support needs improvement so users can better understand generated content, check accuracy, and continue asking questions when feedback is unclear.

Figure 4 shows that all five participants would return to quiz generation, making it the clearest high-value feature in the product. This supports keeping quiz generation visible in the redesigned information architecture. At the same time, the post-test comments show that quiz usefulness depends on better AI support, which is why our prototype emphasizes a more accessible AI Tutor chat instead of a one-time answer flow.

The qualitative quotes in Table 1 show that the redesign decisions are based on repeated user concerns rather than preference alone. The calmer green visual system responds to comments about the original interface feeling too purple or not sophisticated enough. The AI Tutor chat responds to requests for a real chatbot tutor with follow-up questions. The Library redesign responds to the need to revisit old tutor conversations, uploads, and generated study materials. Together, these changes target learnability, efficiency, feedback quality, and long-term usefulness.
