Research Evidence

Methodology, Findings, and Analysis

This page documents the usability data behind the redesign recommendations.

Methodology

We conducted six moderated usability sessions lasting approximately 45 to 60 minutes each. Participants joined remotely, shared their screens, and were asked to think aloud while completing tasks. The research team observed behavior, recorded task completion, and took notes on confusion, hesitation, and repeated actions.

Participants represented several academic roles: one dual enrollment student, three undergraduate students, one graduate student, and one professor or staff member. Each participant completed a short pre-test questionnaire about academic role, study habits, use of digital study tools, and familiarity with AI tools.

Each participant completed seven tasks: find onboarding support, create a study guide, generate a quiz, review quiz results, generate a summary, organize study materials, and use the AI assistant. The tasks were selected because they represent the core workflows StudyX.ai promotes to student users.

We tracked task success, time on task, wrong navigation attempts, quiz feedback ratings, and post-test comments. This combination of quantitative and qualitative data allowed us to identify which interface problems were repeated across users rather than based on a single opinion.

Findings

Finding 1: First-time users lacked a clear starting point

Only one of six participants completed the onboarding task without assistance. Most users paused on the home screen because the interface showed an upload area but did not explain the available tools, what file types were useful, or what a first-time user should do first.

Finding 2: Upload feedback was missing

All six participants expressed uncertainty after selecting a file. The product did not show a progress bar, confirmation message, or processing status. Two participants uploaded the same file again because they believed the first upload had failed.

Finding 3: The sidebar created navigation errors

The sidebar listed more than ten tools with similar visual weight. Four of six participants clicked the wrong tool before finding the quiz or AI assistant feature. The lack of grouping made it difficult to distinguish study creation tools from writing tools and settings.

Finding 4: Quiz feedback was too limited

Only three participants found the quiz results page without help. Participants who found it said the feedback was not detailed enough because it showed whether answers were right or wrong but did not explain the related concept or point users back to the uploaded document.

Finding 5: Organization tools were difficult to discover

Only two of six participants completed the organization task. Participants expected course folders, labels, or a workspace model, but the interface did not clearly show where saved study materials should go. This problem would likely become worse for returning users with many files.

Analysis

Figure 1 shows that the onboarding task had the lowest completion rate, with only one of six participants succeeding. This indicates a learnability problem rather than a preference issue. Because users could not easily understand what StudyX.ai offered, our redesign adds a clear welcome area, a short explanation of the product, and primary calls to action for starting a study task.

Figure 2 shows that upload-dependent tasks took a long time, especially quiz generation at 168 seconds. Participant comments explain that the delay was not only technical; users were unsure whether the system was working. For this reason, our prototype adds a progress bar, upload status text, and completion confirmation so users receive immediate feedback.

Figure 3 shows that users made repeated wrong clicks in the sidebar. This supports a redesign of the navigation, not just a visual style change. The prototype groups features into Home, Library, AI Tools, and Preferences so users can scan by purpose instead of reading a long list of similar tool names.

Figure 4 shows that quiz feedback received an average rating of 4.8 out of 10. The low score is supported by quotes from participants who wanted explanations. Our prototype changes the quiz results component so incorrect answers include an explanation, a reference to the source material, and a prompt to continue with the AI tutor.

The organization task also showed a mismatch between user expectations and the interface. Students expected course-based folders because that model is common in Canvas, Google Drive, and Notion. Our redesign introduces a Library page with course folders and a visible New Course action to make saved materials easier to manage over time.
